Thermal Sciences Laboratory

EML 3301L — Five experiments in fluid mechanics, heat transfer, and thermodynamic systems

Lab 1
Pipe Loss Lab

The objective of this experiment is to characterize frictional losses in a piping system for cooling applications by determining the head losses, friction factors, and pipe roughness associated with two straight copper pipes of differing diameters, as well as the loss coefficient of a 90° elbow. In addition to solving the values of the above parameters, an uncertainty analysis using statistical and Root Sum Squared (RSS) methods will be carried out to determine the accuracy of results. This experiment characterizes the relationship between flow rate and pressure loss and evaluates the effect of pipe geometry and surface roughness on energy dissipation in internal flows.

Lab 1 Report — Pipe Loss Lab Full lab report with friction factors, head loss analysis, and uncertainty calculations
Lab 2
Fan Performance Lab

The objective of this experiment is to evaluate the performance characteristics of two centrifugal fans with different impeller blade geometry — one with radial blades and the other with backwards-inclined blades — operating at multiple speeds. Specifically, the lab aims to determine the relationship between head rise, volumetric flow rate, shaft power, and efficiency for both fans. Dimensionless performance coefficients corresponding to head, power, and flow will be calculated to assess similarity behavior between the two fans operating at varying speeds and air flow conditions for each data set.

Lab 2 Report — Fan Performance Lab Full lab report with performance curves, efficiency analysis, and dimensionless coefficients
Lab 3
Heat Exchanger Lab

The objective of this experiment is to characterize the performance of the departmental double pipe heat exchanger for potential research applications. Specifically, the Log Mean Temperature Difference (LMTD) will be evaluated for both parallel and counter-flow configurations, and the overall heat transfer coefficient area product (UA) is going to be determined experimentally. These values will be compared to theoretical predictions obtained using empirical heat transfer correlations. Additionally, the fouling factor will be estimated, axial temperature distributions will be analyzed, and the uncertainty in UA for each flow configuration is going to be quantified to establish the thermal capability and reliability of the apparatus for future research use.

Lab 3 Report — Heat Exchanger Lab Full lab report with LMTD analysis, UA calculations, and fouling factor estimation
Lab 4
Cooling Tower Lab

The objective of this experiment is to characterize the thermal performance of a packed column cooling tower through analysis of heat and mass transfer between air and water streams. This experiment evaluates the variation in air humidity ratio and relative humidity between the inlet and exit conditions, predicts the exit water temperature using a first law energy balance, and determines the rate of heat dissipation within the system. The measured results are compared to analytical predictions to assess the accuracy of the governing thermodynamic relationships and the effectiveness of the cooling tower under varying water flow rates and heating conditions.

Lab 4 Report — Cooling Tower Lab Full lab report with heat and mass transfer analysis and energy balance calculations
Lab 5
Design and Optimization of a Hydraulic Oil Delivery System for Industrial Press Operation

This project presents the design and evaluation of a hydraulic piping system for delivering and returning oil to a hydraulic press while meeting specified performance metrics. The objective was to ensure 30 to 40 gpm delivery of oil at 50 psia, select an appropriate pump, and design a return system capable of operating under gravity while maintaining a maximum elevated tank volume of 10 gallons. The analysis applied conservation of mass and energy principles through the extended Bernoulli equation, along with Darcy-Weisbach and minor loss correlations, to determine system head requirements and flow behavior. A required pump head of approximately 107 ft at 40 gpm was calculated and used to select a suitable centrifugal pump that operates safely within its performance range and avoids cavitation. The return line was sized as a 2-inch schedule 40 PVC pipe, shown to provide sufficient flow under gravity without an additional pump, and an overflow inlet was recommended to maintain consistent fluid levels and prevent air entrainment. A diaphragm valve was selected for effective flow control, and a cost analysis confirmed the chosen configuration balances performance and economic efficiency.
